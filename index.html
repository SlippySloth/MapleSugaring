What is maple sugaring?

Maple sugaring is the practice of collecting sap from a maple tree, and boiling off most of the water until you are left with a sweet, delicious syrup. There's nothing quite like the taste of that first drop of maple syrup after a season of sugaring.

Sugar content of maple syrup

Proper maple syrup has a final sugar content of 66-67%. If the sugar content is lower (more water), it has a higher tendency to mold. If the sugar content is higher (less water), then the syrup has a tendency to crystallize. That said, if you're final syrup is too thick or too thin, you will still have a tasty product.

Best trees to tap

Many species of maple trees can be tapped for sap. Sugar maples are most commonly used because the raw sap typically has 2% sugar content. However, this can vary widely according to that season's weather, the soil the tree grows in, and other factors. Other trees can be tapped, but will need to be boiled longer.

Maple sugaring season

Maple sugaring season happens when the days are above freezing and nights are below freezing. This is due to pressure. When temperatures get warmer, it creates positive pressure in the tree, which expels the sap out of any holes in the tree. When temperatures go below freezing, it creates negative pressure in the tree, drawing water up from the roots. This replenishes the supply of sap in the tree so there's more sap to flow tomorrow.
